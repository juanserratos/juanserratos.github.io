
### Millennials and the FIRE Movement

This title is meant to be clickbaity in nature, but it is done very poorly. There is, however, a pervasive thought I have about financial markets that we all have to believe in if we want to avoid working until the moment we are on our deathbeds. That is, we need to, in essence, believe in the S&P 500 or, more broadly, the United States financial markets.

It has become more in vogue to consider retirement at earlier and earlier ages, largely as a result of alienation (in Marx's sense). Take, for example, the *FIRE movement* (FIRE stands for "Financial Independence, Retire Early"; see the [FIRE subreddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/Fire/)). This movement is based on living frugally earlier in life in order to maintain high savings rates and aggressively invest those savings with the goal of having enough assets to cover living expenses and, well, retire. The FIRE movement is largely attributed to millennials in the 2010s. There are even funny terms in the FIRE movement, like *Geo-Arb*, which basically means earning money in a strong currency such as the dollar or pound sterling and living in a place with a low cost of living: the digital nomad lifestyle. The peak millennial who, I think, had a large influence on Gen Z high schoolers was Frank Niu (on TikTok). He is a self-proclaimed FIRE advocate and was one of the main people advocating the relaxed SWE lifestyle. His advice on TikTok contributed significantly to the saturation of computer science degrees, I strongly believe.

But more on how the movement actually works. Central to the FIRE movement's thesis are high savings rates at an early age. The idea here, of course, is to get compounding returns at a young age because linear savings alone will be insufficient. For example, consider an \$80,000 after-tax salary starting at age 24, and suppose you are able to save \$25,000 a year until age 37. There are too many other factors to consider across this age range, such as having kids, so we'll stick to these 13 years. Then we will have saved 13 * \$25,000 = \$325,000, which is certainly not enough to retire from age 37 onward. Even if you were able to save around 50 percent of your income during this time period, which is practically not feasible right now, you would have saved \$520,000, which is still not enough to last for the rest of your life. For the FIRE movement, saving linearly is simply not enough to achieve the goal of retiring early. Everyone will point out that, historically, the S&P 500 has delivered an average annual return of approximately 10 percent (see, for example, [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/trading-investing/sp-500-average-return)). The FIRE movement will point to this fact, as will most financial advisors and well-established institutions, and tell you that this is the most important aspect: capturing the 10 percent yearly compounding growth of a broad-based index equity fund like the S&P 500. If our portfolio buys shares of the S&P 500 every year using our yearly \$25,000 contribution from age 24 to 37, then its future value is
$$
FV = P \times \frac{ (1+r)^n - 1}{r}, \; \; P = 25,000, \; r = 0.10, \;n = 13
$$ 
which gives $FV = 613,067.78$. Voila: 188 percent above simple linear savings. This is meant to capture the magic of investing, especially investing at younger ages, since the growth is exponential in the $n$ term. However!! I want to challenge this optimism in the investment-growth thesis, although, spoiler, I still believe in it.
