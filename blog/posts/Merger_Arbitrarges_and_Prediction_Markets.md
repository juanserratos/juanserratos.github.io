At the head of this new company is David Ellison. This *merger* also had to clear regulatory approval, likely with some help from the Trump admin not feeling the need to press on long-time Republican and Trump mega donor Larry Ellison's new ventures. Largely today Paramount looks the same as it was: struggling in the subscription-based media landscape, competing against giants like HBO Max and Netflix.

### Merger Arbitrage and Prediction Markets

This is where we get more to the financial heart of this post, which is more what I wanted to talk about. Kalshi, an American regulated prediction market, which operated as the only one in the US after Polymarket got booted from the operating in the US, got its start, like a lot of us, at Goldman Sachs. Goldman has a lot of trading and structuring desks which focus on something you may have never thought about — I worked on the SPG desk for a good chunk of my summer so this is where I'm coming from. A big part of SPG was  creating specific baskets (this one not so foreign) of financial offerings to more carefully track and have exposure to a specific investment idea, e.g. a basket which specifically has exposure and tracks California's new mandate on electric locomotives. These financial products can get complex and tedious, so the founder's of Kalshi (which means *everything* in Arabic) wanted a way to more directly get exposure to something: by betting on Yes and No contracts on that something (a.k.a binary markets). Although this has evolved to be more *degenerate*, like betting on what words [Bryan Johnson will say during his acid trip](https://polymarket.com/event/what-will-bryan-johnson-say-while-tripping-balls), the original idea was something more like getting direct exposure to whether or not the Bank of Japan will raise interest rates at their next meeting. 

The idea of this post is to actually think about original purpose of these prediction markets. When a merger or acquisition comes into play, like Paramount's acquisition of Warner Bros, the stock price will come to reflect that acquisition offer (given that it seems likely that the board and company will accept). However, there is a "hidden" risk! Warner Bros, in this after hours moment, 11:50 PM EST 02/26/2026, is trading at \$28.80. But what gives, Paramount offered \$31 a share so why are we hypothetically able to profit \$31-28.80 = \$2.20 per share when this acquisition closes. This difference here is largely attributed to regulatory approval and other sorts of risks that come with an acquisition, e.g. a deal fallout. We should note here that a company cannot just back away from a takeover bid like the form Paramount has been proposing. A famous case of this not working out well was Elon Musk's takover of Twitter where he offered to purchase it for \$43 billion, or \$54.20 per share (... going for a 420 joke), but afterwards got slightly cold feet and then was taken to court where he was essentially forced to purchase it. 

Suppose we were to bet on Paramount closing out its acquisition of Warner Bros. We buy \(n \) many shares of Warner Bros stock so that our portfolio on this bet and  current capital looks like \( C_n= \$ n \cdot 28.80 \). Our bet here is on Paramount closing out its acquisition completely, so getting past regulatory approval, etc, and our profit would be \(p_n = \$(31 - 28.80) n  = \$ 2.20 \cdot n \). This would be an ignorant bet, however, given the risks, and history shows that a distatorous breakup of an acquisition (especially on the regulatory level) will have an extreme impact on stock price. If Warner's takeover were to not work out, we could see its stock go down to \$15 dollars per share, so we would want to hedge our bet here with another financial offering. This is where the original purpose of prediction market comes into play. Currently, the market [Who will successfully take over Warner Brothers?](https://kalshi.com/markets/kxtakeoveracqwb/warner-brothers-acquisition/kxtakeoveracqwb-27jun30) offers a direct hedge against this risk, specifically the market "None before July 2027", which stipulates that "if qualifying public announcements indicate no one's takeover of Warner Brothers has succeeded Before July 2027, then the market resolves to Yes." Netflix has walked away  so this is bet on whether or not the Paramount deal will fully close. We can, in this moment, purchase 696 contracts at 17 cents at a total cost of \$128 and have a payout of \$715 (this is just meant to be illustrative, but still a real orderbook order). To mitigate a loss of capital will our stock bet, we can purchase these contracts, which are very cheap.
